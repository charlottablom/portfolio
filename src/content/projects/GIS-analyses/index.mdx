## Context

Bergsjön, in Östra Göteborg, needs a new preschool - but where? The analysis works through that systematically: estimating how many children live in different parts of the district, then narrowing to sites that are actually viable given real-world constraints.

## Methods

Using **QGIS** alongside the **Place Syntax Tool (PST)** plug-in, the analysis layers several criteria on top of each other.

<LoadingImage src={workflow} width={1600} alt="Workflow diagram showing the analysis process: overview of data and data types, data limitation, data processing, overlap analysis, and presentation, each with the specific QGIS operations used." />

Barriers rule out land that's unsuitable for any preschool, regardless of demand: major roads and industrial areas (with a 100-metre buffer, to keep distance from traffic and noise), terrain steeper than 20 degrees, and existing buildings (an 8-metre buffer, so nothing existing would need to be demolished).

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={barriers} width={1600} alt="Map showing barriers to preschool development across Östra Göteborg: major roads, industrial areas, and terrain steeper than 20 degrees, each shown with its buffer zone, overlaid on existing buildings." />
  <figcaption>Barriers to preschool development: major roads, industrial areas, and steep terrain.</figcaption>
</figure>

Separately, **PST**'s attraction-reach function measures how many public transit stops fall within a 500-metre walk of each street segment - the distance most people are willing to walk, per **Gehl** (2010).

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={transit} width={1600} alt="Map showing streets across Östra Göteborg color-coded by how many public transit stops are reachable within a 500-metre walk, using the Place Syntax Tool's attraction-reach function." />
  <figcaption>Public transit proximity: streets within a 500-metre walk of a stop.</figcaption>
</figure>

The same method, repeated with existing preschools as the attraction target instead of transit stops, identifies areas currently underserved - then narrowed to where the most children already live or are expected to live within five years.

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={preschoolProximity} width={1600} alt="Map showing street segments that are both within 500 metres of transit and further than 500 metres from any existing preschool, overlaid with areas shaded by expected number of children." />
  <figcaption>Streets within reach of transit but underserved by existing preschools, shaded by expected number of children.</figcaption>
</figure>

## Outcomes

Overlapping all these layers - viable land, transit access, distance from existing preschools, and proximity to areas with the most children - narrows the district down to three candidate sites. Site 1 is the strongest match: large enough for 352 children against a projected need of 209, and a short walk from the Rymdtorget tram stop. Its main drawback is that it currently sits on forested land, so building there would mean clearing trees. Sites 2 and 3 are smaller and don't fully meet local demand, but show what the method finds elsewhere in the district.
