## Context

The project started from a renovation scenario for **Angeredsgymnasiet**, a school building from the 1980s in Angered, a Gothenburg district that has long been a focus for social investment. The aim was to use co-creation methods to propose a socially, ecologically, and economically sustainable transformation of the existing building - developed together with **Astrid Nathorst Westfeldt**, **Sadia Sharmin**, and **Chiara Lancellotti**, and the students of class SA20 at the school.

Early mapping asked students and teachers to weigh the same set of spaces against different questions - a place to study, a disliked place, a place to spend free time - to find where their answers overlapped.

## Methods

An initial workshop identified eight focus areas that students and teachers highlighted as particularly important: the classroom, common room, corridor, toilets, schoolyard, entrance, group rooms, and the Kunskapsportalen, an open study environment. Each was treated as an "acupuncture point" - a small, well-placed intervention expected to ripple outward across the wider school, following **Jaime Lerner**'s (2014) theory of urban acupuncture.

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={acupuncture} width={1200} alt="Four-step diagram showing small red squares in a white frame multiplying and spreading outward until they cover most of the frame, illustrating how small interventions can generate effects across a larger area." />
  <figcaption>Urban acupuncture: small interventions rippling outward across a larger area.</figcaption>
</figure>

Eighteen students from class SA20 took part across five further workshops, each testing a different way of drawing out their ideas: sketching, a place-making card game scored for social, ecological, and economic sustainability, group negotiation, 3D collage, and a shared physical model of the school.

The place-making game let students "play" different changes to their chosen focus area, each move card-rated for how sustainable it was.

For the 3D collage workshop, students built small paper models of their focus area, folded from a single collaged sheet.

Across the five workshops, students' own words shaped the brief directly - from wanting greenhouses and gardening space in the courtyards to bigger windows, better lighting, and artwork of their own making on the classroom walls.

## Outcomes

Each focus area carried its workshop feedback into a finished design. The corridor, for instance - identified as dark, boring, and central to how the school's grid-like plan actually gets used - gained lockers, seating, and perforated walls that borrow light without losing privacy between corridor and bathroom.

The project closed with a public exhibition, where students presented their ideas to visitors and gathered feedback on the proposals built from their own workshops.

## Reflection

Working through co-creation meant the brief itself was never fixed in advance - it changed with what the workshops surfaced, from the choice of focus areas onward. Treating each intervention as a small, well-placed "acupuncture point" rather than a single comprehensive redesign made it possible to act on very different kinds of feedback - from a request for bigger windows to a wish for somewhere to garden together - without needing them to resolve into one unified scheme.
