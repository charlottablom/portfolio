## Context

The brief came from **Hogia**, a software company on the coast at Stenungsund, following a workshop with its employees. Focused tasks, they said, often went just as well - sometimes better - from home. What they missed was the spontaneous meeting with a colleague: long a source of energy and creativity in the company's close-knit culture, and hard to replace remotely.

Stage one of the headquarters - a split-level building in three floors, built into the site's slope with the sea to the south - became the proposal's focus, covering its entrance and top floors. Large glazed sections give the rooms a bright, airy quality, though some of that quality had been furnished away over time by shelving and screens blocking sightlines. Existing shafts and columns, both within the exterior walls and freestanding inside, set real constraints on where the design could touch the building's structure.

## Methods

Customers and employees move through the building differently - a customer arriving for a meeting follows a fixed sequence from reception to a waiting area to the meeting itself, while employees move more freely between workspaces. Mapping both journeys against the existing plan made clear where a shared social space could sit at their intersection.

<LoadingImage src={circulation} width={1600} alt="Diagram of the entrance floor showing two circulation paths in different colors: one for Hogia staff moving between lounge, meeting rooms, and workstations, and one for visiting customers moving from entrance to reception to waiting areas and meeting rooms." />

The building's daylight has its own logic: spaces along the perimeter are strongly lit, while a core at the building's center stays darker, and the south-facing glazing lets in strong direct sun that can be as disruptive as it is pleasant. Timber screens filter that light before it reaches the working spaces closest to the facade, warming and softening it. The material and furniture proposal built on that logic - end-grain timber flooring, glass-block partitions, and clear and frosted glass, chosen to shape light as much as space.

<LoadingImage src={materials} width={1200} alt="Material and furniture collage: green glass-block tiles, a woven wood ceiling with a ribbon light fixture, a timber wall pattern, and a curved yellow modular sofa with rounded stools." />

## Process

A corridor between the building's east and west halves reads today as a bottleneck. The proposal removes that feeling with a new glazed volume, in the same outward-leaning language as the existing entrance facade, added to both floors it touches. On the entrance floor, this volume becomes a lounge: a slate floor breaking from the rest of the plan, a curved sofa shaped around the existing column structure, and a mature tree brought up through a hole in the floor slab from ground level. Meeting rooms of varying size and enclosure - in timber and glass-block - sit close by, along with the café and exhibition near the entrance.

<LoadingImage src={groundFloor} width={1600} alt="Color-coded entrance floor plan showing zones for reception, café, exhibition, meeting rooms, and the new lounge extension with its curved seating." />

On the top floor, the same glazed extension becomes a roof terrace, its position between two projecting building volumes giving it both a wind-sheltered corner and a permanently shaded one - a place for conference mingling, for lunch, or for working outside. It sits between a new demo room for showing customers Hogia's products interactively and the building's largest conference room.

<LoadingImage src={upperFloor} width={1600} alt="Color-coded upper floor plan showing the demo room, main conference room, office landscape, and the roof terrace positioned between two building wings." />

Reorganizing the stage this way shifts its area distribution significantly - office space shrinks while meeting rooms, the café, the lounge, and the terrace all grow.

<LoadingImage src={areaChart} width={1400} alt="Stacked bar chart comparing area distribution before and after the proposal, showing office space shrinking and meeting rooms, café, lounge, and terrace all growing." />

The extension's facade is restored to Hogia's original blue, breaking from the yellow of the building's other stages - a small, deliberate way of helping visitors find the right entrance among several similar buildings.

<LoadingImage src={section} width={1200} alt="Cross-section through the building showing the relationship between floors in the new extension, with the roof terrace, lounge, and ground-floor tree visible, and the facade shown in its restored blue color." />

## Outcomes

The lounge is the project's clearest expression of the brief: a tree lit from below, its shadows cast on the ceiling, glass-block that reads like moving water when lit, and organically shaped furniture continuing the natural materials Hogia has long used elsewhere in the building.

<LoadingImage src={lounge} width={1600} alt="Rendered interior of the lounge showing a person walking past a glass-block wall, a tree with dappled shadows, and the curved yellow sofa beyond a glazed stair opening." />

Near the entrance, the café gives arriving customers somewhere to sit with a coffee before a meeting, or employees somewhere to meet informally between other tasks.

<LoadingImage src={cafe} width={1600} alt="Rendered interior of the café near the entrance, with timber-slatted walls, round tables, a yellow armchair, and a frosted glass-block partition." />

And on the terrace, the same emphasis on nature carries outdoors - sea air, the sound of reeds, and a view that was already the building's strongest asset, now given somewhere to sit and use it.

<LoadingImage src={terrace} width={1600} alt="Rendered exterior of the roof terrace with two people seated under a retractable awning, overlooking the water, with lavender planted in large pots along the railing." />

## Reflection

Working with the rooms of work means making spatial interpretations of comfort and engagement from an organization's own wishes and needs. Here, that meant redefining a conventional workplace to meet a way of working that emerged from the pandemic - and the workshop we ran with Hogia's employees was central to that: it shaped both how we understood their current work patterns and what they wanted from the building's future.

With that in mind, we chose to differentiate stage one from the rest of the complex, making it a center for employees to meet and a place to welcome new customers into the company. Along the way, we learned to design with the building's available light directly - testing different forms of artificial light to draw out new materials and forms, and exploring what happens when the two are combined.
