## Context

Many of Gothenburg's suburban areas face social exclusion, car dependency, and poor connectivity within the urban street network. The thesis investigates how strategic urban planning can create social-ecological synergies - and surface the conflicts - in the city's periphery, using north eastern Gothenburg as a case study. It was tied to **Evolutive Meshed Compact City (EMC²)**, a **Vinnova**-funded research project led by **Chalmers' Spatial Morphology Group (SMoG)**.

GIS analyses mapped the social networks that structure how people move through the city - street centrality by pedestrian and vehicular flow, layered against built functions like housing, healthcare, and schools.

## Methods

EMC² proposes an alternative to the standard 15-minute city model for car-dependent suburbs. Rather than spreading density evenly, it concentrates growth along a single redesigned main street - keeping everyday destinations within a 15-minute walk without fragmenting the green corridors around it.

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={cityModel} width={1600} alt="Diagram comparing the 15-minute city model in a generic suburban grid against the same grid with the EMC2 model applied, showing densification concentrated along one main street connecting two destinations, with habitat corridors preserved around it." />
  <figcaption>The 15-minute city model in a generic suburban grid, and the EMC² model applied to it.</figcaption>
</figure>

Overlaying the social and ecological networks surfaced both conflicts, where dense human activity and high habitat value collide, and opportunities: two parallel strategic plans, one linking suburbs through social exchange along the main street, the other linking green corridors through ecosystem services at the city scale.

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={strategicSocial} width={1400} alt="Strategic plan diagram showing suburbs in north eastern Gothenburg linked in a loop by social exchange along a proposed main street." />
  <figcaption>Strategic design linking suburbs through social exchange along the main street.</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={strategicEcological} width={1400} alt="Strategic plan diagram showing green corridors in north eastern Gothenburg linked through ecosystem services, connecting the river, wetlands, and parks." />
  <figcaption>Strategic design linking green corridors through ecosystem services at the city scale.</figcaption>
</figure>

Four design principles carried that analysis into a densification proposal: suburban typologies keep their existing openness to green space, but gain entrances that face the street instead of the private courtyard; a clear street hierarchy balances urban activity against quieter courtyards; density follows centrality, to keep businesses viable along the main street; and multifunctional green structure runs between buildings, providing both recreation and ecosystem services.

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={designPrinciples} width={1400} alt="Four diagrams illustrating design principles: suburban typologies open to green space with street-facing entrances; a clear street hierarchy; density following centrality; and integrated multifunctional green structure between buildings." />
  <figcaption>Four design principles carried from analysis into the densification proposal.</figcaption>
</figure>

## Outcomes

The result is a social-ecological case study revealing synergies and conflicts between urban and natural systems in Gothenburg's periphery. Building on that analysis, the thesis proposes a redesigned network of main streets for the area, plus a detailed local design for one of them: Kvibergs bäckväg, a street crossing the Kvibergsbäcken stream and linking the peripheral districts of Kortedala, Kviberg, and Utby.

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={sitePlan} width={1600} alt="Site plan for Kvibergs bäckväg, showing the redesigned street network and building layout linking Kortedala, Kviberg, and Utby, with a locator map inset." />
  <figcaption>Site plan for Kvibergs bäckväg, linking Kortedala, Kviberg, and Utby.</figcaption>
</figure>

The street's character shifts along its length. Near the public square, high foot traffic supports cafés and everyday street life:

<figure>
  <LoadingImage src={perspective2} width={1600} alt="Rendered street-level perspective of a tree-lined plaza near a public square, with colorful building facades, outdoor seating, and people walking, cycling, and sitting at tables." />
  <figcaption>Street level near the public square.</figcaption>
</figure>

Where it crosses the stream, the design steps back instead - a quieter boardwalk with informal seating, positioned to stay out of the way of the stream's ecological function rather than displacing it, as shown at the top of this page.

The same multi-species thinking carries down to the scale of individual buildings, integrating homes, food, and foraging opportunities for people and other species directly into the architecture.

## Reflection

Ecological research tends to focus on species that are well documented and easy for people to notice and relate to. Capturing ecological value more fully means looking beyond what's visible, to the fuller range of species and senses through which a place is actually experienced. The thesis also points to the importance of including human residents as stakeholders alongside the non-human species it modeled, since different communities can perceive and value ecological richness differently.
