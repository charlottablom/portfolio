## Context

The project began with a deliberately loose way of choosing a site: closing our eyes and zooming in on a map of Gothenburg. It landed just south of Kyrkbyn, bordered by a large green space to the west and **Volvo**'s factories beyond. The tower's twisting form responds to the undulating shapes of nearby buildings, but reinterprets that language through rotation along its vertical axis.

## Methods

We imagined the tower as a fully-glazed office for **Volvo**'s security-classified R&D - needing to exclude nearly all daylight and shield its interior from outside view. In **Grasshopper**, we scripted a kinetic facade of panels that rotate around vectors drawn from each corner of a facade module, letting each panel close completely or extend outward from the surface by a controlled amount. The result is a facade that can reconfigure itself entirely depending on the need for privacy, darkness, or daylight - closer to a chameleon than a fixed skin.

## Outcomes

We tested the facade at both extremes. Fully closed, the building admits very little daylight on its northeast-facing side - workable for some activities, potentially uncomfortable for others. Fully open, sunlight reaches deeper into the core, though the middle floors stay comparatively shaded, a side-effect of the tower's rotation putting its widest face toward the north.

We also compared outward views in both states. With the panels mostly closed, the clearest sightlines run through the middle of the building; move closer to the facade and the panel angle becomes far more critical to what you can actually see. With the panels open, view quality improves substantially and much more evenly across the building - even its lowest measured value comes in well above the closed version's best case.
