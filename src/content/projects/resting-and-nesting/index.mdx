## Context

Green roofs are usually judged by what they do for people or for stormwater. This project asks what one designed as deliberately for **fauna** as for **humans** would look like — a place to nest as much as to rest.

The site is a proposed medium-density development (5–8 storeys) near Uppsala, bordered by open agricultural land, mixed coniferous forest, and a green corridor left behind by a set of powerlines.

Four species anchor the design, each declining and each plausibly able to use a roof: the **Northern Lapwing**, a ground-nester losing wet meadows to modernized agriculture; the **Common Swift** and **Common Starling**, both cavity-nesters squeezed out by modern roofing and urbanization; and the **Great yellow bumble bee**, losing nesting ground as agricultural edges are drained and overgrown.

## Methods

Not every building in the development is an equally good candidate for a biotope roof. Overlaying four criteria — disconnection from nature, existing green structure, stormwater need, and demand for recreation — across the building volumes made it possible to single out the strongest case for one.

<LoadingImage src={methodsPlacement} width={1600} alt="Hand-drawn diagram overlaying four wavy bands (disconnection from nature, green structure, stormwater management, need for recreation) across a row of building volumes, with one building circled in red as the strongest candidate." />

The chosen building's foraging ranges were checked against each species: the Northern Lapwing forages within 2 km, the Common Starling within 1 km, the Great yellow bumble bee within roughly 400–950 m, and the Common Swift much further, 48–61 km — its chicks independent from the moment they leave the nest.

Translating that into the roof itself meant mapping how differently each species moves once there — a human wandering the deck, a swift arriving only in flight, a bumblebee tracking scent across the sedum, a lapwing walking the open ground.

<LoadingImage src={methodsAxonometric} width={1600} alt="Axonometric sketch of the roof with drawn paths showing how a human, common swift, great yellow bumble bee, and common starling would each move across and use the same roof." />

## Process

Precedents for wild, low-maintenance planting on constructed ground — a dry meadow at Arla in Vimmerby, the sunken pools at Operaparken, and Nathalie Baumann et al.'s research on green roof biotopes — informed a set of named, sized zones stepped across the roof: Mountain, Allotment, Dry Meadow, and Forest Edge.

<LoadingImage src={processBiotopes} width={1600} alt="Three precedent photographs of dry meadow and wetland planting, beside an axonometric sketch of the stepped roof labeled with zones: Mountain, Allotment, Dry Meadow, Forest Edge, and Open Field." />

Each zone drew on a shared toolkit — trampling-tolerant vegetation for well-used paths, bird bricks in the parapets, biochar and reused building material as substrate and cavities, and perennials of varying height for insect shelter. Built into a labeled roof section, these run from a bird tower terrace and allotment boxes at one end to increasingly uneven sedum tussock and dry meadow at the other.

<LoadingImage src={processElements} width={1600} alt="Five material photographs — trampling-tolerant vegetation, bird bricks, binoculars, biochar, and obsolete building material — beside a labeled axonometric roof section showing bird bricks, a bird tower terrace, allotment boxes, and a gradient of planting toward a dry meadow." />

Two cross-sections test the same gradient in elevation — a recreational deck, a band reserved for maintenance access, then an increasingly wild edge — cut at two points along the building to check the logic holds.

<LoadingImage src={processSections} width={1600} alt="Two building sections, A-A and B-B, each showing a gradient from a recreational roof terrace, through a maintenance zone, to a wild planted roof edge." />

## Outcomes

The resolved plan wraps four biotope roofs — Mountain, Permaculture, Dry Meadow, and Open Field — around a student apartment building with an aquaponic system below. Each carries its own maintenance regime, from mowing the dry meadow to leaving the open field's sand mounds undisturbed for ground-nesting bees.

<LoadingImage src={outcomeRelations} width={1600} alt="Plan of the building with four biotope roofs marked in color — Mountain, Permaculture, Dry Meadow, and Open Field — each annotated with its required maintenance." />

At 1:200, the planting plan sets out the species behind each zone: dry meadow perennials like Achillea, Campanula rotundifolia, and Dianthus deltoides; mountain species such as Betula nana and Calluna vulgaris; permaculture shrubs and fruit like Aronia melanocarpa and Ribes nigrum; open field ground cover dominated by Sedum and Festuca.

<LoadingImage src={outcomePlan} width={1400} alt="Detailed 1:200 roof plan with individual plants labeled by species across the four biotope zones, including a legend of shrub and perennial names." />

Two construction details work out what sits beneath the planting. On the five-storey dry meadow roof, the build-up runs waterproofing membrane, root barrier, drainage layer, and 200–300 mm of substrate — 10% biochar, 70% crushed bricks, 10% native plants, 10% green compost — against a lighter, deeper mix for the mountain and permaculture roofs above.

<LoadingImage src={outcomeDryMeadow} width={1600} alt="Building section through the five-storey dry meadow roof, elevation view, with substrate specifications for the mountain, permaculture, and dry meadow zones and the plant species used at roof level." />

On the seven-storey open field roof, a shallower 80–200 mm substrate of biochar, pumice, native plants, and green compost carries a sparser, more open ground cover — reflecting the lighter load allowed higher up the building.

<LoadingImage src={outcomeSectionDD} width={1600} alt="Building section D-D through the seven-storey open field roof, showing the substrate build-up and low ground-cover planting at roof level." />

Across the four roofs, the planting palette runs to more than forty species, chosen throughout for drought tolerance and value to the four focus species rather than appearance — from dry meadow perennials like *Leucanthemum vulgare* to mountain specialists like *Thymus serpyllum*, permaculture fruit like *Aronia melanocarpa*, and open field ground cover built mostly from *Sedum* and fescue.

## Reflection

Some of what the roof needs is straightforward: most plants are drought-resistant once established, though seeded germination needs closer tending; spontaneous vegetation must stay clear of the areas set aside for sand-nesting insects and bumblebees; and safety harnesses are required wherever a parapet is under 1,100 mm.

Other constraints are harder to design around. Water on a roof is complex — volume must stay sufficient and circulating to avoid algae, and storing it risks the structure below without expert input. Much of the roof is reachable only by stairs, so tools and equipment have to be carried up rather than lifted. And because the roofs serve different goals — some recreational, some built for biodiversity — that distinction needs to be communicated clearly to residents and maintenance staff, or it risks being lost.

Building height and accessibility limit which species can realistically use a given roof, and railway noise complicates its other role as a place to rest. Replacement habitats like these also carry a structural risk: without enough surrounding habitat to draw from, a biotope roof can become an ecological sink rather than a net gain. Further investigation would look at how the roof might be colonized by local flora over time, at seeding methods — plug plants, bare-rooted stock, direct seeding, or hay transfer — and at how a longer-term maintenance plan and public access could be organized once it's in use.
