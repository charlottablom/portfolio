## Context

A multi-family apartment building in five storeys, its pitched roof and banded facade wrapping a mix of one- to four-room apartments around a shared stair core.

## Methods

Each floor pairs apartments of different sizes - from a 26 m² one-room unit to a 104 m² four-room unit - around a single stair, mirrored across the building's width so every combination of unit sizes repeats floor to floor.

Alternative combinations of those same units were tested against the fixed stair core, regrouping which apartments share a floor without changing the building's overall structure.

## Outcomes

Once the design was resolved, **Johanna Åström** and I ran a full energy balance calculation against it. Sweden's building code (BBR) sets a maximum specific energy use of 85 kWh/m²A<sub>temp</sub> per year for this climate zone; without heat recovery, the building reaches 78.1. With a heat exchanger, that drops by almost 30% to 57 kWh/m²A<sub>temp</sub> per year - inside the code requirement, though just above the 50 kWh/m²A<sub>temp</sub> threshold for classification as a low-energy building.

Windows turned out to be the building's weak point: despite covering a small share of the facade, they account for 41% of transmission losses and 43% of total energy use - more than the walls, which make up far more of the envelope's area. Doubling the window area alone pushes heating energy use up substantially; doubling it while also doubling the window's U-value pushes it further still. Lowering the indoor set point from 22°C to 21°C, by contrast, only trims energy use modestly - a reminder that the envelope, not the thermostat, is what this design lives or dies by.
