## Context

Kalmar's central station sits on **Kvarnholmen**, a historic island and the region's biggest transport hub. Three problems make that placement hard to hold onto: capacity that can't meet the city's expected growth, three railroad crossings that fence central Kalmar off from itself, and an elevation of just 1.5 m above sea level — putting the station among the first parts of the city exposed to storm surges as the climate changes.

<LoadingImage src={site} width={1600} alt="Map of central Kalmar showing the comprehensive plan, projected 100-year flood extent under climate scenario SSP 5-8.5 by 2100, the current Kalmar C at +1.5 m, and the proposed Kalmar C 2.0 at +8.0 m in Gamla industriområdet." />

## Methods

The response is a three-part transformation. Train traffic relocates to a new **Kalmar C 2.0**, built at +8.0 m in **Gamla industriområdet**, aligned with existing plans for large-scale development there under the New European Bauhaus initiative. The old straight rails are reused at lower speed for "soft" mobility — trolleys, promenades, and bike tracks connecting Kvarnholmen to destinations like Baronen and Linnaeus University, removing the barrier the fenced railway once created. And the site of the old Kalmar C becomes **flexmark**: flexible ground handed over to mobile structures once the trains are gone.

<LoadingImage src={diagram} width={1600} alt="Transit-map-style diagram of the shore line corridor, running from Kalmar C 2.0 through Systraströmmen and Kalmar C to Kvarnholmen, with branches to Baronen, Linnaeus University, Sylvanderparken, and the Guest Harbor, each line labeled with its promenade, bicycle track, or mobile structures." />

## Process

At the old station, gravel parking lots and railway macadam are reused as a sand park, both recreational and sporty; where asphalt remains, it's cracked open to let vegetation back in.

<LoadingImage src={sandPark} width={1400} alt="Watercolor of people playing boule on a gravel sand park beside a rail trolley track, with blue flowers, poplars, and a station building in the background." />

The planting itself — Salix, sunflowers, reed — does double duty: room-bounding while the flexmark is in use, and able to accumulate cadmium, zinc, and lead, common pollutants in railway ground, setting up the phytoremediation the site will need later.

<LoadingImage src={section} width={1600} alt="Labeled roof section of a mobile hostel unit and mobile playground, with a restaurant beyond and Salix, sunflowers, and reed planted alongside the track." />

## Outcomes

By 2050, the old Kalmar C has become a mobility park stitching Kvarnholmen to the new centre emerging at Gamla industriområdet: a sun beach, volleyball and boule courts, a kiosk, mobile sauna and sun decks, and a Salix promenade running beneath a Populus avenue.

<LoadingImage src={masterplan2050} width={1600} alt="Masterplan of the mobility park in 2050, showing a sun beach, volleyball court, sand park, boule court, mobile sauna and sun decks, kiosk, and a tree-lined promenade running along the old rail line to the Guest Harbor." />

Cycling the Populus avenue is one way to reach Gamla industriområdet — the other is on foot along the same promenade, or by trolley.

<LoadingImage src={populusAvenue} width={1200} alt="Watercolor of a cyclist riding along a rail-trolley track lined with tall poplar trees." />

At just 1.5 m above sea level, the mobility park is still at risk of periodic flooding within a hundred years. So the design is built to retreat: nothing added is fixed in place to be destroyed by a future storm surge. As the water arrives, the same Salix, sunflowers, and reed that once bounded the park's rooms go to work extracting heavy metals from the soil, keeping them from reaching the Baltic.

<LoadingImage src={masterplan2100} width={1600} alt="Masterplan of the same site in 2100 under 100-year flooding, showing the former mobility park submerged and the mobile hostel, sauna, and sun decks retreated along the rail line toward Gamla industriområdet." />

The commute changes with it. By trolley, it's roughly 1.5 km to Gamla industriområdet — flat, and scenic across the flooded ground. Head the other way instead, and the trip ends with a dip in the sea and a dry-off in the public sauna.
